# DriftQCap

**DriftQCap** is an evaluation protocol and benchmark artifact for few-shot quantum capability prediction under distribution shift.

Quantum capability models are often evaluated under static train-test splits. DriftQCap instead evaluates whether a source-trained capability predictor remains useful when the deployment setting changes: device noise, calibration state, circuit depth, circuit family, or multiple factors may shift between source training and target use. The protocol combines structured source-to-target episodes, explicit target-label budgets, label-acquisition policies, and calibration diagnostics.

This repository contains the anonymized code and data interface for reproducing the DriftQCap paper results.

## Data Splits

The public release contains three CSV splits.

### 1. Controlled synthetic benchmark

```text
dataset_release/synthetic/driftqcap_synthetic_paper_rf_v3.csv
```

This is the primary controlled benchmark. It contains source and target episodes across five operational shift families:

- noise shift
- depth shift
- circuit-family shift
- temporal shift
- compound shift

The controlled split is designed for reproducible stress-testing of few-shot adaptation under known shift mechanisms.

### 2. Telemetry-anchored semi-synthetic split

```text
dataset_release/semi_synth/semi_synth_from_snapshots_clean.csv
```

This split uses IBM backend calibration/noise telemetry as device-state seeds. Circuit structures and labels are generated by the DriftQCap pipeline conditioned on those telemetry values.

This split should be described as **telemetry-anchored semi-synthetic**, not as full executed-hardware validation.

### 3. Thin executed-hardware pilot

```text
dataset_release/real_hardware/real_eval_with_synthetic_source.csv
```

This split contains a limited executed-hardware pilot schema-aligned with the synthetic source split. It is intended as a sanity check and should not be used as the primary benchmark evidence or as a device-ranking dataset.

## Evaluation Protocol

DriftQCap evaluates source-to-target transfer under explicit target-label budgets:

```text
K ∈ {8, 16, 32, 64}
```

For each target episode, a source model is trained on source episodes only. At evaluation time, a small target subset of size `K` is selected using a label-acquisition policy. The remaining target circuits are held out for evaluation.

The protocol evaluates:

1. zero-shot transfer from source to target,
2. few-shot adaptation under target-label budgets,
3. label-acquisition efficiency,
4. calibration and conformal interval reliability,
5. episode-level statistical robustness.

All main statistical claims should be computed at the **episode level**, not at the raw circuit-row level.

---

## Reference Baselines

The pipeline includes standard adaptation and comparison baselines:

- source-only transfer
- coarse source-only transfer
- target-only fitting
- pooled retraining
- mean-shift bias adjustment
- shuffled-label control
- residual adapter family

The residual adapter family is used as a **reference adaptation baseline**, not as the central contribution of the repository. DriftQCap’s contribution is the reusable evaluation setting: structured drift episodes, explicit budgets, label-acquisition comparison, calibration diagnostics, and reproducible splits.

---

## Label Acquisition Policies

The repository includes several deployable label-acquisition policies, including:

- random selection
- diversity-based selection
- shift-aware diversity
- stratified diversity
- uncertainty-based selection
- uncertainty-trimmed selection
- hybrid explore-exploit policies

The paper reports that diversity-based selection is generally more stable than uncertainty-based selection under the evaluated drift conditions. The interpretation should be made in terms of representativeness and model-misfit diagnostics, not as a universal claim about all active-learning methods.

---

## Calibration and Conformal Evaluation

DriftQCap evaluates calibration separately from point error. The pipeline reports metrics such as:

- mean absolute error
- expected calibration error
- conformal interval coverage
- budget-averaged performance

This distinction is important because a method may improve point prediction error while still producing unreliable confidence estimates under shift.

## Hardware and Telemetry Notes

This repository includes two hardware-related components:

1. **Telemetry-anchored semi-synthetic data**  
   Uses real IBM backend calibration/noise telemetry as device-state covariates, while circuit structures and labels are generated by the DriftQCap pipeline.

2. **Thin executed-hardware pilot**  
   A limited sanity-check split derived from small executed-hardware runs.

Neither component should be interpreted as a full multi-backend hardware validation benchmark. The primary benchmark evidence is the controlled synthetic evaluation, with telemetry-anchored and pilot results used for grounding and sanity checks.

## Known Limitations

DriftQCap is intended for evaluating capability-prediction robustness under controlled drift. It should not be used for:

- vendor ranking,
- procurement decisions,
- safety certification,
- claims of guaranteed performance on unseen quantum devices,
- claims of full real-hardware validation.

Known limitations include:

- the primary benchmark is synthetic,
- the telemetry-anchored split is semi-synthetic,
- the executed-hardware pilot is intentionally small,
- the circuit families and shift mechanisms do not cover all possible quantum workloads,
- physics-aware architectures such as qpa-NN can be integrated into the protocol but are not claimed as fully reproduced here.
